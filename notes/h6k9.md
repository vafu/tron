Question/hypothesis: Can the connected IR cameras expose manual control over IR
sensor exposure so IR brightness can be made predictable enough to use as a
rough depth signal.

Observed result: Host V4L2 devices are hidden in the default sandbox, but
escalated `v4l2-ctl --list-devices` sees NexiGo HelloCam IR on `/dev/video51`
and Lenovo Performance Camera IR on `/dev/video55`. `/dev/video55` exposes
standard UVC `auto_exposure` with menu values `1: Manual Mode` and
`3: Aperture Priority Mode`, plus `exposure_time_absolute` range `3..500`.
`/dev/video51` exposes GREY capture but only ROI controls through standard
V4L2; no standard exposure control appeared in `--all` or
`--list-ctrls-menus`.

Follow-up tasks or instrumentation gaps: Test whether `/dev/video55` actually
holds manual exposure during streaming; investigate `/dev/video51` UVC extension
units for vendor-specific exposure/emitter controls.

Question/hypothesis: Treat IR strobing as already-understood ambient rejection
and focus specifically on fixed/manual IR sensor exposure.

Observed result: `/dev/video55` accepts standard V4L2 exposure control:
`v4l2-ctl -d /dev/video55 --set-ctrl=auto_exposure=1,exposure_time_absolute=50`
puts the IR stream in manual mode, and `--get-ctrl` reports manual mode with the
requested exposure value. Restored it to the original state afterward:
`auto_exposure=3`, `exposure_time_absolute=50`.

Observed result: `/dev/video51` does not expose standard V4L2 exposure controls.
`--list-ctrls-menus` shows only ROI controls, and direct attempts to set
`auto_exposure` or `exposure_time_absolute` return `unknown control`.

Observed result: The NexiGo sibling node `/dev/video49` exposes standard
exposure controls, but changing `/dev/video49` from manual exposure 1 to 1250
did not materially change `/dev/video51` IR stream mean brightness. `/dev/video49`
was restored to `auto_exposure=3`, `exposure_time_absolute=156`.

Follow-up tasks or instrumentation gaps: For a predictable brightness-depth
experiment, use `/dev/video55` first because it has direct manual exposure. For
`/dev/video51`, the only likely remaining path is vendor-specific UVC extension
unit probing/mapping; standard V4L2 controls are absent on the IR node and the
RGB sibling controls do not appear to drive the IR sensor.

Question/hypothesis: Does `/dev/video55` standard manual exposure produce a
controlled IR brightness response suitable for brightness-as-depth experiments.

Observed result: User observed the live stream still appears to compensate when
a hand moves closer, despite `auto_exposure=1`. Controlled raw captures with the
viewer closed confirm this concern. With `backlight_compensation=0` and
`auto_exposure=1`, 30-frame GREY captures at `exposure_time_absolute=3` and
`500` did not produce a simple monotonic fixed-brightness response:

- exp 3: overall mean 52.12, frame-mean range 22.70..92.81.
- exp 500: overall mean 59.94, frame-mean range 3.02..176.05.

The control reports manual mode and accepts exposure values, but another stage
still causes large frame-to-frame intensity changes or compensation in the IR
stream. Restored `/dev/video55` to `auto_exposure=1`,
`exposure_time_absolute=100`, `backlight_compensation=1`.

Follow-up tasks or instrumentation gaps: Search for hidden gain/ISP/emitter
controls or metadata. Test whether disabling/controlling IR strobe/emitter makes
exposure response predictable. For brightness-depth calibration, compute
brightness from strobe-on/off paired differences or per-phase frames rather than
raw frame mean until the remaining automatic stage is identified.
